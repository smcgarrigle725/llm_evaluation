SURRENDER CHARGE SCHEDULE (Illustrative Example)

A surrender charge is a fee imposed by an insurance company when a
policyholder withdraws funds from an annuity contract beyond a permitted
free withdrawal amount, particularly during the early years of the
contract. Surrender charges are designed to allow the insurer to recover
costs associated with issuing the contract, including distribution and
commission costs.

Standard Surrender Charge Schedule (7-Year Example)

Contract Year      Surrender Charge Percentage
Year 1                      7%
Year 2                      7%
Year 3                      6%
Year 4                      5%
Year 5                      4%
Year 6                      3%
Year 7                      2%
Year 8 and beyond           0%

The surrender charge is generally calculated as a percentage of the amount
withdrawn that exceeds the free withdrawal amount, not the full contract
value.

Free Withdrawal Amount

Most contracts permit a free withdrawal each contract year, typically up
to 10% of the contract value (or, in some products, 10% of purchase
payments) as of the most recent contract anniversary, without incurring a
surrender charge. Amounts withdrawn beyond the free withdrawal allowance
during the surrender charge period are subject to the applicable surrender
charge percentage shown above.

Waiver of Surrender Charge

Surrender charges are commonly waived under certain circumstances,
including:

- Death of the policyholder (the death benefit is paid without surrender
  charge).
- Annuitization of the contract after a minimum holding period (commonly
  five years), depending on contract terms.
- Confinement to a licensed nursing home for an extended period, if the
  contract includes a nursing home waiver rider.
- Terminal illness, if the contract includes a terminal illness waiver
  rider.

Market Value Adjustment (MVA)

Some annuity contracts, particularly those with a fixed-rate component,
include a Market Value Adjustment in addition to or instead of a
traditional surrender charge. An MVA adjusts the amount paid upon
surrender or withdrawal based on the change in interest rates between the
date the purchase payment was made and the date of withdrawal. If interest
rates have risen since the purchase payment was made, the MVA will
typically reduce the amount paid to the policyholder; if interest rates
have fallen, the MVA may increase the amount paid.
